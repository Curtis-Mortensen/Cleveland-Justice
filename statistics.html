The Problem

This fall, BLM protests captured the world's attention. However, before justice became a national movement Cleveland has long had a history of systemic problems. A 2015 US Department of Justice Review found that "it's a mess", and had stayed a mess since a 2005 review. Let's review the history of the Cleveland Police Department.

When did all the problems start? Presumably after the economic collapse

Which brings us to the Modern Era. There is no more damning evidence then the report published by the government itself, after the US Department of Justice was forced to investigate the Cleveland Department of Police or CDP. After the DOJ published a 59 page report called “Investigation of the Cleveland Division of Police'' they then stated that the Cleveland Police were the first police department to have been found "insufficient" in all categories.

Since that document was largely the inspiration for creating this website, I wanted to include it here. The DOJ found that “CDP engages in a pattern or practice of using unreasonable force”. The Department of Justice continued to say that this was “in violation of the Fourth Amendment'' of the US Constitution. 

Here’s a summary of key points from this investigation. All of the text is from the original document, but has been given emphasis with bold face and summarization. Any of my own text has been added in italics. Examples from the investigation are quoted. 

The department began this investigation in March 2013… in March 2011, the Cleveland Plain Dealer began analysis of the CDP's use of force and, over the next several months, ran a series of articles that describe significant problems with the CDP's force practices, based on a review of publicly available data. 

On November 29th, 2012, over 100 Cleveland police officers engaged in a high-speed chase, in violation of CDP policies, and fatally shot two unarmed citizens ...13 CDP officers ultimately fired 137 shots at the car, killing both its occupants...

In actuality, we found that during the time period we reviewed the officers were only suspended for any period of time on approximately six occasions for using improper force. Discipline is so rare that no more than 51 officers out of a sworn force of 1,500 were disciplined in any fashion in connection with the use of force incident over a three-and-a-half year period. However, when we examined discipline numbers

further, it was apparent that in most of those 51 cases the actual discipline imposed was for

procedural violations such as failing to file a report, charges were dismissed or deemed

unfounded, or the disciplinary process was suspended due to pending civil claims. A finding of excessive force by the internal disciplinary system is exceedingly rare. A member of the

Office of Professional Standards (or which, among other duties, has been charged with

investigating use of deadly force incidents, stated that the office has not reviewed a deadly force incident since 2012.

The current pattern or practice of constitutional violations is even more troubling because we identified many of these structural deficiencies more than 10 years ago during our previous investigation of CDP's use of force. In 2002, we provided initial observations regarding CDP's use of force and the accountability systems and, in 2004, we recommended that the division make changes to address some of the deficiencies we identified. CDP entered into an agreement with us, but that agreement was not enforced by a court and did not involve an independent monitor to assess its implementation. The agreement did require CDP to make a variety of changes, including revising its use of force policy and establishing new procedures for reviewing officer involved shootings. In 2005, we found that Cleveland had abided by that agreement and it was terminated. It is clear, however, that despite these measures many the policy and practice reforms that were initiated in response to our 2004 memorandum agreement were either not fully implemented or, if implemented, were not maintained over time."

We reviewed… nearly 600 reports and investigations of officers use of force covering a three year period. ...We… provide two or three examples that illustrate each of the problems that we found… [but] these examples are far from an exhaustive list of the incidents that we found that violate the constitution. 

The report gives 15 categorical failings of the police department, in 3 different categories, each supported by examples. Here’s the key points and stories. 

Category 1: CDP officers engage in a pattern or practice of using unreasonable force

1. GDP officers shoot at people who do not pose an imminent threat of serious bodily harm or death to the officers or others.

“Anthony” [name changed] was being held against his will inside a house by armed assailants. When officers arrived on scene, they had information that two armed assailants or holding several people inside the house. After officers surrounded the house, Anthony escaped from his captors and ran from the house, wearing only boxer shorts. An officer ordered Anthony to stop, but Anthony continued to run toward the officers. One sergeant fired two shots at him… No other officers at the scene reported seeing Anthony point anything at the sergeant. The sergeant's use of deadly force was unreasonable. It is only by fortune that he did not kill the crime victim in this incident. 

2. CDP officers hit people in the head with their guns and situations where the use of deadly force is not justified. 

3. CDP officers use less legal force that is disproportionate to the resistance or threat encountered. 

  1. Head and body strikes

  2. Tasers and OC spray

An officer used his taser to stun a 127-pound juvenile twice as two officers held them on the ground. The officers allege that the 127-pound juvenile "continued to resist" as they both held him on the ground, prompting one of the officers to deploy his taser twice in the juvenile’s back in drive stun mode, even though both officers were holding him down. 

4. CTP officers use unreasonable force, including tasers, against individuals with mental illness, individuals in medical crisis, and individuals with impaired faculties. 

Category 2: CP officers commit tactical errors that endangered the Cleveland community and reduce officer safety as well

  1. CDP officers carelessly fire their weapons, placing themselves, subjects, and bystanders at unwanted risk of serious injury or death. 

  2. CDP officers use other dangerous and poor tactics, placing members of the Cleveland community at risk.

Category 3: Systemic deficiencies cause or contribute to the excessive of force

  1. CDP does not ensure that officers adequately report the force they use

  2. Supervisory investigations of force are inadequate

In the board's review, investigators are not invited to attend meetings. Additionally, the board has inexplicably instructed investigators not to include an officer's prior complaint and disciplinary history in the investigative file. 

  1. CDP's internal review mechanisms are inadequate

    1. CDP fails to adequately investigate and hold officers accountable for misconduct

    2. CDP fails to adequately investigate civilian complaints of officer misconduct

CDP will not accept anonymous or third-party complaints.

  1. CDP officers are inadequately supported and trained

  2. CDP's use of force policy is still deficient

  3. CDP's early intervention system is inadequate

  4. CDP is not engaging in community policing effectively at all levels of the division

Members of racial, ethnic, and language minorities expressed public outrage at the way they perceive that their communities are treated by CDP. Reports of the enormous amount of force that culminated in the fatal shooting of two unarmed African-Americans in East Cleveland on November 29th 2012; the revelation that officers were caught on tape kicking an African-American man in the head, who was handcuffed, prone on the ground, and appears to have surrendered, and then did not report having used any force; claims by a Latino family that officers chased and forcibly handcuffed their teenage son who has Down syndrome while looking for robbery suspects who did not resemble the boy...

During our tours, we additionally observed that neither command staff or line officers were able to accurately or uniformly describe what community policing is or how CDP implements a community policing model. Our review revealed in addition to the programmatic aspects of community policing, that CDP's community policing strategy, at least until recently, has consisted of three elements: The bureau of community policing and district community service units that perform limited community policing functions; supervisors instructing patrol officers to get out of their zone cars and walk around the community while providing little additional guidance or training; and monthly CDP sponsored community meetings that do not appear to attract the members of the community who have the most strained relationships with the police. 

  1. CDP's approach to individuals in crisis is underdeveloped

CDP must change its policies and procedures to require CIT officers to respond to every incident involving an individual and known mental health crisis. CDP’s policy currently requires two officers to respond to calls involving individuals with mental illness. According to the documentation provided by CDP, however, it is common for only one CDP officer to respond to such a call. 

CDP policy currently limits the definition of "crisis" in the mental health context to situations where there is no law violation. CDP must expand this definition. 

  1. CDP equipment, technology, and staff planning are inadequate

CDP's failure to appropriately allocate resources including staffing and equipment contributes to the pattern or practice of unconstitutional force. In addition, Cleveland police officers are not given the basic equipment, the physical structures, and the technology required to perform their jobs safely and effectively. 

The city and division must work together to ensure that limited resources are allocated thoughtfully and effectively. At the 4th district, for example, there was until recently only one computer for all Patrol officers ...Because of the poor condition of CDP's vehicles, officers told us that some of them carry auto repair equipment and other maintenance materials which they have purchased at their own expense.
